## Problem Statement

AI engineers who design multi-agent systems for clients or internal use-cases have
no tool that connects a visual canvas to ownable, version-controlled LangGraph code
on a hosted runtime. They either write raw LangGraph (slow, no canvas), use no-code
tools (Flowise, Gumloop, Lindy — black-box, you hit a ceiling and churn to code), or
bolt LangChain scripts onto general automation like n8n (no store-context awareness,
no AI-native design surface). The gap is the *connection* of three altitudes —
prompt → canvas → code — as one round-tripping artifact.

## Demand Evidence

- **Founder-as-user:** runs/handles an ecommerce store; lives with the "multiple
  redundant Shopify plugins to solve a store-specific automation" pain.
- **Inbound signal:** other ecommerce operators ask the founder whether they "know
  how to use n8n" and about AI email automation (envying competitors who have it).
  Diffuse but real.
- **Named individual:** founder says they can name one specific human (not supplied
  in session). **Action: write it down this week.**
- **Honest baseline:** self-assessed "conviction, no proof yet," source = "a reasoned
  market bet" — then revealed to have lived ecommerce pain underneath. Warm, not hot.
  Zero users, zero design partners, zero paying customers as of this session.

## Status Quo (the real competitor)

A ~15-person ecommerce/services company that wants AI customer service today does one
of: copy-paste tickets into ChatGPT; hire a consultant to bolt a LangChain script onto
Zendesk/Gorgias; stack redundant Shopify plugins for store-specific workflows; reach
for n8n as a general automation tool (not store-context-aware); or do nothing because
it's too hard. Cost: plugin subscription bloat, manual triage hours, and the inability
to get an agent that "understands how to automate in the context of *our* store."
**n8n is the status quo *and* the incumbent OSS/template-marketplace model.**

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

- **ICP:** the AI engineer who designs multi-agent systems for clients or use-cases
  (agency/consultant flavor + internal platform engineer). Reaffirms `WEDGE-PLAN.md`;
  supersedes the "generic SMB-RAG-builder" framing.
- **Wedge:** the round-trip canvas — build visually, drop into ownable LangGraph code
  the moment you hit the ceiling, stay on the hosted runtime. "AI builds it, canvas
  lets you trust it, code lets you own it" — one artifact, three altitudes.
- **Validation beachhead (not product scope):** ecommerce-adjacent AI engineers. Where
  the founder's signal is warmest. Targeting a wedge inside a horizontal product, not
  narrowing the product.

## Constraints

Pre-product, solo. MVP built through Phase 5 (canvas, versioned DSL, LangGraph
compiler, hosted runtime, RAG ingestion, tools + ReAct, agent types, Reflexion loop,
Better Auth). LangGraph/LangChain as a platform dependency (keep compiler behind an
interface). LLM margin is the #1 business risk. The five-day strategy-churn pattern
(4 positions in 5 days) is a process risk.

## Premises (confirmed this session)

1. **ICP = AI engineer, horizontal.** Ecommerce is the validation beachhead, not the
   product scope. *(Founder rejected narrowing; accepted.)*
2. **Open source is a distribution/positioning layer, not the wedge.** The wedge is
   the AI-native round-trip canvas + ownable code on a hosted runtime. *(Accepted.)*
3. **Stay-and-extend runtime, not export-and-leave.** *(Consciously chosen over the
   "export to your own app" framing. Each implies different retention/revenue.)*
4. **Validate before building solutions on top.** Marketplace and full-OSS-launch
   presuppose demand that is not yet verified. *(Accepted as a sequencing constraint.)*

## Approaches Considered

**A — Validate-first, OSS-later (`WEDGE-PLAN.md` as written):** blind code panel +
10–15 design partners; OSS the core only after retention is proven. Safest, slowest
distribution.

**B — Open-source-now (canvas + DSL + compiler), launch HN/PH, adoption = demand
signal:** highest variance, fastest distribution if it lands, public-flop risk.

**C — OSS the DSL + codegen only; closed-beta canvas + runtime (CHOSEN):** captures
OSS credibility/funnel on the cheapest standalone piece, keeps the differentiating
canvas closed while validating demand honestly via a small closed beta.

## Recommended Approach — C

Open-source **just the versioned DSL + LangGraph code generator** as a standalone
library — the literal embodiment of "code is the receipt," useful on its own (a
versioned graph spec → LangGraph code). Keep the canvas + hosted runtime in a
**closed beta of ~10 design partners** recruited from the founder's warm
ecommerce-adjacent network. The OSS DSL is top-of-funnel: engineers adopt the spec in
their own projects and graduate into the paid canvas. Marketplace deferred to G2 per
[`MVP.md`](./MVP.md).

**Rationale:** honors the founder's stated OSS conviction without betting the company
on a public launch that can't be redone; keeps demand falsification honest and
observable (you can watch 10 people hit the ceiling); embodies the open-core "Git
playbook" — open the trust-building artifact, charge for the experience around it. A
is safe but leaves OSS-distribution value on the table for this ICP; B is a
high-variance bet the founder hasn't earned the right to spend yet.

## Open Questions

- Is the DSL standalone-useful enough as OSS to draw adoption without the canvas,
  given LangGraph Studio + LangSmith exist? What's the "use the spec alone" hook?
- Precise open-core boundary: open = DSL + codegen + node registry? Paid = canvas,
  runtime, copilot, collaboration, evals? Needs a concrete line.
- License: Apache-2.0 (permissive, max adoption) vs AGPL-3.0 (protects the hosted
  layer from cloud competitors) — a real open-core decision.
- Who are the 10 named design partners? Founder owes the names.
- Does the OSS DSL launch need its own mini-GTM (a demo, a "why a spec" post), or is
  it pure credibility?

## Success Criteria (from `WEDGE-PLAN.md`)

- Blind code-quality panel ≥ 70% "would-merge," ≥ 4/5 mean.
- ≥ 10 design partners with a real agent running.
- Code-drop rate ≥ 30%; ceiling-event resolution ≥ 50% continue; 30-day agent
  retention ≥ 40%.
- ≥ 25 paying workspaces, positive gross margin per run, ≥ 1 segment converting ~3×
  others — by Day 90.

## Distribution Plan

- **OSS DSL layer:** GitHub repo, Apache or AGPL (decide before publish), a standalone
  "graph spec → LangGraph" README + one killer demo. Modest launch (show HN),
  low-stakes and redo-able.
- **Closed beta canvas + runtime:** direct-recruit 10 from warm network; hand-onboard;
  watch them build one real agent each. No public launch until Phase 1/2 gates pass.
- **Paid hosted runtime + copilot + collaboration:** turn on after retention is proven
  (`WEDGE-PLAN.md` Phase 3). Existing `apps/web` + `apps/api` deployment path covers it.

## Post-script (2026-06-23, later that day)

After this session, the founder clarified a larger **north-star vision**: Calypr as
"the agentic layer" — a marketplace of full agent-powered apps, each shipping with a
configurable agentic backend and a tweakable front-end UI design system. AI engineer is
the supply side (creator); the app-buyer is the demand side.

This is recorded as the **destination**, not a change to the near-term wedge decided
above. Reasoning: a two-sided marketplace and a front-end app builder are oceans, and
both are unreachable unless the round-trip canvas + hosted runtime are proven first.
The vision makes the runtime MORE load-bearing, not less — so the ICP, the
stay-and-extend bet, the open-core model (Approach C), and the ecommerce validation
beachhead all stand. The near-term plan is unchanged; the vision is sequenced as a
"vision ladder" (Rungs 0–5) in [`ROADMAP-6M.md`](./ROADMAP-6M.md). This is also the
**fifth** strategy framing in five days; the discipline of treating it as a destination
rather than re-opening the wedge is itself the test of whether the churn risk
(identified above) is being managed.
